When Does Refinancing Your Mortgage Actually Make Sense?

Learn the math behind the break-even point to decide if a new loan is the right move.

The Break-Even Point Calculation

Refinancing isn't free. You'll pay 2% to 5% of the loan amount in closing costs. To see if it's worth it, divide the total cost of the refinance by your monthly savings. If the refinance costs $6,000 and you save $200/month, your break-even point is 30 months. If you plan to stay in the home longer than that, it makes sense.

Rate-and-Term vs Cash-Out Refi

A rate-and-term refinance simply changes your interest rate or loan duration. A cash-out refinance allows you to tap into your home's equity, giving you cash for repairs or debt consolidation. Be careful: cash-out refinances usually come with higher interest rates and more debt.

When NOT to Refinance

If you plan to sell your home in the next two years, you won't reach your break-even point. Also, be wary of "no-closing-cost" refinances—these usually just roll the costs into a higher interest rate, which can cost you more in the long run.

Refinancing to Remove PMI

If your home has appreciated significantly, you might be able to refinance to a new loan that is less than 80% of the home's current value. This allows you to drop Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) without needing a 20% down payment from your original purchase.

Step-by-Step Example

On a $300,000 loan, dropping your rate from 7% to 6% saves about $200 a month. If the refinance costs $5,000, you'll need to stay in the home for 25 months to recover the costs. After month 25, you are purely "profitable" from the move.
