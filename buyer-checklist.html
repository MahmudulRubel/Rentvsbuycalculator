First-Time Homebuyer Checklist (2026)

Generate a step-by-step roadmap to guide you through the homebuying process, customized to your credit score, budget, and state.

Roadmap Filters

Your Overall Roadmap Completion 0%
1

Financial Preparation

0 / 5 Done
Pull statutory copies from AnnualCreditReport.com. Audit reports for any filing discrepancies or late entries.
Evaluate monthly recurring debts to target an overall housing debt-to-income (DTI) ratio below 36%.
Aim for 3% to 20% down, and budget 2% to 5% of purchase price in additional liquid cash to pay closing costs.
Secure 3 to 6 months of living expenses in savings so you aren't completely drained when you close escrow.
Organize 2 years of W-2s or 1099 tax filings, 30 days of paystubs, and 2 months of full bank statements.
2

Mortgage Pre-Approval

0 / 5 Done
Identify which mortgage best matches your down payment and eligibility (Conventional, FHA, VA, USDA, state grants).
Apply to traditional banks, online mortgage brokers, and local credit unions to source competitive rates.
Provide verification assets to your chosen lender to receive a locked maximum pre-approval amount letter.
Do NOT apply for auto loans, open credit cards, or run large balances while pre-approved; it can cancel your loan.
Ask your lender about local housing agency down payment grants or municipal tax credits.
3

House Hunting

0 / 5 Done
Outline must-have school criteria, neighborhood safety, grocery proximity, and commute transit limits.
Hire an experienced, licensed real estate agent who specializes in first-time buyer negotiations.
Browse local listings on MLS, set automated notifications, and tour neighborhoods in person.
Inspect structural disclosures and county assessments for water leaks, mechanical wear, or high taxes.
Drive from serious home listings to your primary office during morning or evening peak hours to test commute limits.
4

Under Contract

0 / 5 Done
Work with your real estate agent to draft a strategic, competitive bid backed by comparative market analysis (CMA).
Submit 1% to 2% of the purchase price to the escrow company as good faith money within 3 business days of acceptance.
Audit the property for foundation integrity, active roof leaks, faulty electrical systems, and plumbing wear.
Request physical repairs or cash credits from the seller based on inspector-identified structural defects.
Finalize your formal loan application paperwork and submit it to underwrite the specific contract property.
5

Closing the Deal

0 / 5 Done
Secure a comprehensive homeowners property policy and send the active binder to your lender before closing.
Lock in your final interest rate with your lender to shield against interest rate spikes before closing day.
Verify your final interest rates, P&I payments, escrow reserves, and out-of-pocket wire costs line-by-line.
Tour the home 24-48 hours before closing to confirm repair updates are completed and the property is in good condition.
Secure bank wire instructions, wire your closing costs, sign mortgage notes, and receive the keys to your new home.

How to Use This Calculator

The First-Time Homebuyer Checklist generates an active, customized roadmap to steer you from prep work to keys-in-hand day. To build your checklist, input your state, target budget, and credit score. The checklist automatically adapts its tasks, adding critical milestones or tips based on your credit tier. The checklist is divided into 5 chronological phases: Financial Prep, Loan Shopping, Home Search, Under Contract, and Closing the Deal. You can mark items off to update active progress rings and bars in real time. The page features print-optimized styles so you can easily print or PDF export your customized roadmap to take on your house hunting journey.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the first step in buying a house? +
The first step is establishing your household budget, checking credit scores, and saving for your initial down payment and closing costs.
Why is getting pre-approved for a mortgage important? +
Pre-approval proves your purchasing eligibility to real estate agents and sellers, locking in loan terms and validating your offer credibility.
What does a home inspector check? +
Home inspectors evaluate the structure, roofing, plumbing, electrical networks, and heating systems to identify potential expensive defects before purchase.
