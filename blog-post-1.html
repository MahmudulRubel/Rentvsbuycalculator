How Much House Can I Actually Afford in 2025?

Expert guide to calculating your home buying power in today's market.

The 28/36 Rule Explained

The 28/36 rule is a classic benchmark used by lenders to determine how much house you can afford. It suggests that your monthly mortgage payment (including taxes and insurance) should not exceed 28% of your gross monthly income, and your total debt payments should not exceed 36%.

Understanding the DTI Ratio

Debt-to-Income (DTI) ratio is critical. For example, if you earn $10,000 per month and have $500 in car payments and $300 in student loans, your existing debt is $800. A 36% DTI limit would allow for a total of $3,600 in monthly debt payments, meaning your maximum mortgage would be $2,800 ($3,600 - $800).

How Lenders Calculate Affordability

Lenders don't just look at income; they evaluate your credit score, employment history, and liquid assets. In 2025, with fluctuating interest rates, lenders are being more conservative with debt ratios to ensure stability.

Hidden Costs That Shrink Your Budget

Don't forget the "extras." Property taxes, homeowners insurance, HOA fees, and maintenance (the 1% rule) can easily add $500–$1,000 to your monthly cost. If you ignore these, you might end up "house poor."

How Interest Rates Affect Purchasing Power

Every 1% increase in mortgage rates typically reduces your purchasing power by about 10%. If you qualified for a $400,000 home at 6%, you might only qualify for $360,000 if rates jump to 7%.

Worked Example: $85,000 Income

With an $85,000 annual income ($7,083/mo):

  • Max Mortgage (28%): $1,983/mo.
  • Assuming 7% rates and 20% down, this equates to a home price of approximately $325,000 depending on local taxes.

Common Mistakes First-Time Buyers Make

The biggest mistake is buying at the absolute maximum of what a bank will lend. Banks don't account for your lifestyle, hobbies, or retirement goals. Always leave a buffer.
