Renting With Pets: What Landlords Won't Tell You

How to find a great home for you and your furry friends without breaking the bank.

Pet Deposits vs Pet Rent vs Pet Fees

Understand the difference. A pet deposit is usually refundable if there is no damage. Pet rent is an ongoing monthly fee (often $25–$100). Pet fees are one-time non-refundable payments. Knowing these terms helps you calculate the true cost of your rental.

How to Negotiate Pet Policies

If a landlord says "no pets," don't always take it as the final answer. Offer a "pet resume" including your pet's breed, weight, vaccination records, and training certifications. Offering a slightly higher security deposit can also sway a hesitant landlord.

The ESA Letter: What's Real vs Not

Emotional Support Animals (ESAs) are protected under the Fair Housing Act. Landlords cannot charge pet rent or fees for verified ESAs. However, you must have a legitimate letter from a licensed mental health professional. Online "registries" are often scams and won't hold up legally.

Breed Restrictions and Insurance

Many landlords restrict certain breeds (like Pit Bulls or Rottweilers) because their master insurance policy won't cover them. If you own a restricted breed, offer to carry your own pet liability insurance policy to mitigate the landlord's risk.

Keeping Your Deposit

To ensure you get your deposit back, take photos of everything before moving in. Use area rugs to protect hardwood floors and keep your pet's nails trimmed. Using an enzymatic cleaner for accidents immediately can prevent permanent odors that lead to total carpet replacement fees.
